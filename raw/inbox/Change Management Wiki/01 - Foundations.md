# Change Management Foundations

## Purpose

Use this page when a change is still being framed. The goal is to define the problem, the reason for change, the scope of impact, and the person accountable for leading adoption.

## Best practices

- Describe the change in plain language before describing the solution.
- State the business driver clearly: cost, speed, risk reduction, compliance, customer impact, or scale.
- Define the size of change using both people impact and system/process impact.
- Name a single change lead who coordinates planning, communications, training, and adoption.
- Document what is known now and update the plan as uncertainty reduces.

## Capture first

- What is changing?
- Why is the change needed now?
- What problem does it solve?
- How many people, teams, systems, or processes are affected?
- What will feel different for end users on day one?
- Who owns the change outcome, not just technical delivery?

## Strong framing statement

A strong framing statement explains:

- the reason for change
- the intended benefit
- the scope of impact
- the accountable owner

Example:

`We are moving the service request process to a shared platform so teams can resolve requests faster, improve visibility, and reduce duplicate manual work across Operations, HR, and IT.`

## Good artifacts

- one-paragraph change summary
- impacted audience list
- initial scope and assumptions
- named change lead
- risk and dependency log

## Common mistakes

- treating change management as an announcement instead of a workstream
- focusing on tool features before explaining the problem being solved
- underestimating how many adjacent teams are affected
- leaving ownership split across too many people

## Review questions

- Could a manager explain this change in two sentences?
- Is the reason for change stronger than "leadership wants it"?
- Have we identified both direct and indirect impacts?
- Is there one person accountable for change adoption?
