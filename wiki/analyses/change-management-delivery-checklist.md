# Change Management Delivery Checklist

## Question

- What are the durable end-to-end steps for planning and delivering organizational change from framing through post-launch reinforcement?

## Answer

- Frame the change in plain language: define what is changing, why now, what problem it solves, who is affected, and who owns the adoption outcome.
- Define the target state: describe the user-visible end state, expected benefits, success metrics, required behavior changes, and the consequences of failure.
- Set sponsorship and governance: name the sponsor, change lead, business owner, technical owner, champions, and go/no-go decision path.
- Choose rollout scope intentionally: decide between phased or big-bang rollout based on urgency, risk, readiness, and support capacity; prioritize critical use cases before broader feature coverage.
- Build an audience-specific communications plan: tailor messages, channels, cadence, and resistance handling for sponsors, managers, highly impacted teams, and support staff.
- Design role-based training and support: define what each audience should know, do, and feel; stand up a learning hub plus visible support channels before launch.
- Run UAT against real workflows: test the MVP with representatives from critical departments, validate integrations and permissions, and set explicit launch blockers in advance.
- Monitor adoption after go-live: track usage, experience, and business signals; review progress at 30, 60, and 90 days; target interventions where adoption lags.

## Evidence

- [[sources/change-management-wiki-bundle]] provides the lifecycle structure behind each checklist step.
- The bundle consistently defines success as adoption and outcome improvement rather than technology deployment alone.
- The source also emphasizes that sponsorship, manager reinforcement, and post-launch monitoring are part of delivery, not optional extras.

## Gaps

- The source does not provide a formal stakeholder-mapping method, a change-readiness scoring model, or a comparative evaluation of named frameworks.
- It is also light on budgeting, incentive design, and sector-specific constraints.
