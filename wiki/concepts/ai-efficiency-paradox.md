# AI Efficiency Paradox

## Summary

- The source uses this idea to argue that AI can improve firm-level efficiency and measured output while making the broader economy less stable.
- In this frame, productivity gains are real, but their distribution matters more than the aggregate number.

## Definition

- AI efficiency paradox: AI adoption raises productivity, margins, and nominal output, but also weakens labor income, consumption, and financial stability enough to offset or reverse the expected macro benefit.

## Supporting Evidence

- [[sources/2028-global-intelligence-crisis]] argues firms initially celebrate margin expansion and reinvest profits into more AI capacity.
- The same process reduces demand for white-collar labor, weakening household income and consumer spending.
- The source treats this as a self-reinforcing loop rather than a short transition shock.

## Counterpoints

- The source is a scenario exercise, not proof.
- It does not establish that new job creation, lower prices, or new categories of demand would fail to compensate over time.
- It is unclear how quickly policy or institutional adaptation could dampen the loop.
