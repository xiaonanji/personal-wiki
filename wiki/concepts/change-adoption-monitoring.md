# Change Adoption Monitoring

## Summary

- Change adoption monitoring is the post-launch practice of checking whether target users are actually shifting behavior, getting value, and sustaining the new way of working.
- The source treats adoption problems as evidence about design, support, readiness, or reinforcement, not as a reason to blame users by default.
- Effective monitoring combines quantitative usage data with qualitative feedback so teams can respond to friction quickly and keep sponsor attention focused on real outcomes.

## Definitions

### Usage indicators

- Signals such as active users, completion of critical workflows, drop-off points, and continued use of legacy workarounds.

### Experience indicators

- Feedback from support tickets, surveys, training follow-ups, managers, and frontline support channels.

### Business indicators

- Outcome measures such as speed, quality, compliance, or reduction in rework once the new way of working is in place.

### 30-60-90 day review pattern

- A staged cadence for detecting early friction, comparing adoption across groups, and checking whether intended value is appearing over time.

## Supporting Evidence

- [[sources/change-management-wiki-bundle]] recommends monitoring both behavior and business outcomes instead of equating logins with success.
- The source also recommends segmenting adoption by audience or department so interventions can be targeted.
- It further emphasizes regular reporting to sponsors and leaders plus visible follow-through on feedback.

## Counterpoints

- Lightweight monitoring can miss deeper structural issues if the selected metrics are too shallow or too delayed.
- Adoption data without local context can mislead teams; low usage may reflect poor process design, insufficient training, or policy misalignment rather than simple user reluctance.
