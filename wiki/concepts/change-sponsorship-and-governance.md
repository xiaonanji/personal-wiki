# Change Sponsorship and Governance

## Summary

- Change sponsorship and governance define who is accountable for adoption, who legitimizes the change, who makes tradeoff decisions, and how blockers are escalated.
- The core idea is that change efforts stall when sponsorship is passive, decision rights are vague, or business voices are missing from rollout planning.
- The source also treats champions and people managers as operational governance actors because they shape whether local teams actually adopt the new behavior.

## Definitions

### Executive sponsor

- Senior leader who creates urgency, reinforces expectations, and removes cross-team blockers.

### Change lead

- Owner of the change-management workstream across communications, training, readiness, and feedback loops.

### Business and technical owners

- Delivery leaders who validate the process design, prioritize scope, and ensure systems or dependencies are ready.

### Champions and managers

- Local amplifiers who test messages, surface resistance, model desired behavior, and translate the change into team-level expectations.

## Supporting Evidence

- [[sources/change-management-wiki-bundle]] calls for visible alignment between delivery ownership and executive sponsorship.
- The source explicitly recommends weekly rollout reviews, a simple RAID log, and a named final decision-maker for launch readiness.
- It also highlights sponsor behaviors that matter in practice: repeated explanation of the why, milestone visibility, and questions about adoption progress rather than only technical status.

## Counterpoints

- A sponsor title alone does not guarantee effective sponsorship; inactive or symbolic sponsors can create false confidence.
- Governance can become counterproductive if it adds ceremony without helping teams resolve scope, risk, or launch decisions faster.
