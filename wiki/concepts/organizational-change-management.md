# Organizational Change Management

## Summary

- Organizational change management is the discipline of helping people adopt a new tool, process, or operating model in a way that produces the intended business outcome.
- In this source branch, it is framed as a full delivery workstream: define the reason for change, make the end state concrete, assign ownership, prepare rollout, support users through transition, and keep measuring adoption after launch.
- Technical implementation is only one input. Success depends on whether people understand the change, see value in it, can perform the new workflows, and stop reverting to old ways.

## Definitions

### Framing

- Clarify what is changing, why it matters now, who is affected, and who owns the adoption outcome.

### Transition design

- Translate the change into rollout sequencing, communications, training, support, and launch-readiness decisions.

### Adoption reinforcement

- Measure whether people are actually using the new way of working and intervene when friction, confusion, or resistance appears.

## Supporting Evidence

- [[sources/change-management-wiki-bundle]] starts with foundations and explicitly warns against treating change management as a late-stage announcement.
- The same source links vision, sponsorship, rollout design, communications, training, UAT, and adoption monitoring as one continuous chain rather than isolated tasks.
- It also defines success in user and business terms, not just in technical-delivery terms.

## Counterpoints

- Not every change requires the same level of formal process; small or low-risk changes can be overburdened by heavyweight governance.
- A concise playbook like this can underspecify stakeholder politics, incentive design, or regulatory obligations in more complex environments.
